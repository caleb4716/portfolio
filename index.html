My Work

Sudoku Game

Sudoku is a logic-based, combinatorial number-placement puzzle. The objective is to fill a 9×9 grid with digits so that each column, each row, and each of the nine 3×3 sub-grids that compose the grid (also called "boxes", "blocks", "regions", or "sub-squares") contains all of the digits from 1 to 9. The puzzle setter provides a partially completed grid, which for a well-posed puzzle has a unique solution.

One of my first real projects in Java, this game allows the player to play the game of sudoku in a window on their desktop. The player is able to start new games, save games, and load games. While in a game, the player has the option of enabling or disabling Error Checking. When Error Checking is enabled, the game will highlight the rows, columns, and mini-grids in which conflicting numbers are found, allowing newer players the opportunity to catch a mistake as it is made, rather than continuing to play and mess up the entire board.

About Me

My interest in computer programming and software development was sparked in my senior year of high school. One of our projects in an engineering principles class revolved around building and programming a robot to perform a basic task. My group of three decided to build a controllable robot tank. The other two were in charge of assembling the robot, and I was placed in charge of programming the controller to drive the tank using a simple C-based programming language. That experience left me curious as to what else we could do, and within the week we hooked up the tank with sensors and were able to have it drive autopilot along a tape path laid out throughout the classroom.

After high school I continued to experimenent around with basic programming projects, toying with Java and C# and a little basic web development on the side. A friend and I considered starting a freelance web design gig, but after looking into more complex web development I decided that I wanted fill in the gaps in my limited knowledge, and decided to enroll at Hennepin Technical College and pursue an Associate's Degree in .NET programming. At the point at which anybody other than my HTML instructor sees this site, I will have completed most if not all of my associates degree and will be planning the next step in my software development career, whether it be an internship, an entry level programming job, or a Bachelor's Degree in Computer Science.
